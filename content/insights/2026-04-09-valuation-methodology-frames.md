Valuation methodology selection affects both purchase price and post-acquisition performance measurement. Buyers who understand methodology selection achieve better outcomes than those who apply inappropriate frameworks. This memo analyzes valuation methodology.

## The Methodology Landscape

Multiple valuation methodologies apply to private company acquisitions. Different methodologies produce different values for the same business. Methodology selection should align with business characteristics and acquisition purpose.

+ Comparable company analysis: values businesses relative to similar public companies.
+ Comparable transaction analysis: values businesses relative to similar private transactions.
+ Discounted cash flow analysis: values businesses based on projected future cash flows.
+ Asset-based valuation: values businesses based on asset liquidation or replacement value.
+ Earnings multiples: values businesses based on earnings multiples adjusted for specific factors.

Methodology selection depends on data availability, business characteristics, and buyer preferences.

## Comparable Company Analysis

Comparable company analysis values businesses relative to similar public companies.

+ Collect relevant public company multiples.
+ Adjust for size, growth, profitability differences.
+ Apply to target business metrics.

The methodology works best for businesses with clear public comparables. Less relevant for niche businesses or unusual business models.

## Comparable Transaction Analysis

Comparable transaction analysis values businesses relative to similar private transactions.

+ Collect relevant private transaction multiples.
+ Adjust for deal characteristics, market timing.
+ Apply to target business metrics.

This methodology often produces the most relevant values for private company acquisitions. Transaction comparables better reflect private market dynamics than public comparables.

## Discounted Cash Flow Analysis

Discounted cash flow analysis values businesses based on projected future cash flows.

+ Project future cash flows for explicit period.
+ Estimate terminal value.
+ Discount at appropriate rate.
+ Add non-operating assets, subtract liabilities.

DCF provides theoretical rigor but depends heavily on assumptions. Best used for businesses with predictable cash flows.

## Earnings Multiple Application

Earnings multiples represent the most common valuation approach in mid-market acquisitions.

+ Select appropriate multiple based on comparables, business characteristics.
+ Apply to normalized earnings measure.
+ Adjust for specific factors: customer concentration, key person dependence, growth profile.

Multiple selection requires judgment about business quality and market positioning. The same business can justify different multiples based on risk and growth profiles.

## Multiple Selection Factors

Multiple selection depends on multiple factors that affect business value.

+ Revenue growth rate: higher growth justifies higher multiples.
+ Profitability: higher margins justify higher multiples.
+ Recurring revenue: recurring revenue models justify higher multiples than project-based.
+ Customer concentration: high concentration reduces multiples.
+ Key person dependence: owner dependence reduces multiples.
+ Market position: market leadership justifies premium multiples.

Factor analysis enables nuanced multiple selection that reflects business-specific risk and opportunity.

## Valuation Range Development

Valuation should produce ranges rather than point estimates.

+ Develop low, base, high scenarios based on methodology variation.
+ Consider sensitivity to key assumptions.
+ Establish walk-away valuation based on required returns.

Ranges acknowledge uncertainty while enabling decision-making. Point estimates create false precision.

## Post-Acquisition Measurement

Valuation methodology affects post-acquisition performance measurement.

+ Track metrics consistent with valuation methodology.
+ Measure value creation against valuation assumptions.
+ Adjust expectations based on actual performance.

Consistent measurement methodology enables accurate performance assessment.
