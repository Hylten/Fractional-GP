---
title: "The Emerging Manager Fiction: Why First-Time Funds Are Not Value"
date: "2026-03-18"
author: "Jonas Hyltén"
description: "A skeptical analysis of emerging manager programs and why they consistently underperform."
---

Emerging manager programs are marketed as LP alpha. LPs receive preferential access to first-time funds at reduced fees. The implication is that LPs are getting in early on the next top-quartile performers. The track record does not support this narrative.

## The Selection Problem

First-time funds are raised by GPs who could not raise from institutional investors. The institutional investors who rejected those GPs made that decision based on analysis. They have more information about GP quality than emerging manager programs provide.

The best-performing GPs do not need emerging manager programs. They have track records that attract traditional LP base. They raise oversubscribed funds. They do not need incentive programs to access capital. The GPs in emerging manager programs are selected by the market as inferior.

This is not universally true. Some excellent GPs start with first-time funds. But the prior probability is against any specific first-time fund being excellent. The selection through emerging manager programs does not improve the probability. It may worsen it.

## The Resource Gap

First-time funds operate with constrained resources. The GP team is smaller. The infrastructure is less developed. The support functions are less mature. These constraints affect every aspect of fund operations.

Deal sourcing suffers. First-time GPs have smaller networks. They have less brand recognition. They have fewer relationships with advisors and intermediaries. This limits access to proprietary deal flow.

Portfolio company support suffers. First-time GPs have less operational expertise. They have fewer portfolio company resources. They have less ability to add value beyond capital. This limits the transformation potential of portfolio companies.

The resource constraints compound. Limited deal flow leads to fewer investments. Fewer investments limit learning. Limited learning constrains future performance. The cycle is difficult to break.

## The Track Record Evidence

Empirical evidence on first-time fund performance is consistent. First-time funds underperform established funds by 200 to 400 basis points. The underperformance persists across vintage years and strategies. It is not explained by sector or geography.

The underperformance has multiple components. Selection explains part. First-time funds are selected from the population of GPs who could not raise traditional capital. Resource constraints explain part. The remaining gap may be attributable to the learning curve that first-time GPs must navigate.

Whatever the cause, the evidence is clear. First-time funds do not generate the returns that justify the risk. LPs would be better served by allocating to established managers with track records.

## The Fee Fiction

Emerging manager programs often include fee concessions. Reduced management fees. Reduced carry. The fee reduction is marketed as LP benefit. It is not.

The fee reduction is small relative to the performance gap. A 1% fee reduction does not compensate for 3% annual underperformance. The LP would be better off paying full fees to an established manager and receiving top-quartile returns.

The fee reduction also distorts LP decision-making. LPs allocate to first-time funds because of the fee incentive. They do not evaluate the fundamental investment merit. The fee becomes a reason to invest rather than a consideration in investment analysis.

## The Recommendation

Emerging manager programs should be treated as marketing. The programs exist to move product, not to create LP value. The track record confirms that LPs would be better served by traditional manager selection.

If an LP wishes to support emerging managers, it should be done through dedicated emerging manager allocation. The allocation should be capped at a small percentage of total private equity portfolio. The expectation should be below-market returns. The benefit is diversification, not alpha.

The default should be skepticism. First-time funds are first-time for reasons. The reasons are related to quality. Quality affects returns. The math is straightforward.
