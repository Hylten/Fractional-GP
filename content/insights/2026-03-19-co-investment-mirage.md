Co-investments are marketed as LP alpha. The GP offers LPs the opportunity to invest alongside the fund in specific transactions. No management fee. No carry. The implication is that co-investments generate superior returns. The data does not support this narrative.

## The Selection Bias Problem

GPs offer co-investments selectively. They do not offer co-investments on their worst deals. They offer co-investments on deals they are confident will perform. The deals they are most confident about are the deals where they have the most alignment with LP interests.

This creates a fundamental misalignment. The GP is offering co-investment on deals where their confidence is highest. Those deals are priced accordingly. There is no pricing advantage. The GP is not leaving money on the table.

Meanwhile, the deals where co-investment is not offered may be the opportunities where the GP has less conviction. Those are the deals where LP capital might actually generate alpha. By offering co-investment on high-conviction deals and keeping low-conviction deals for the fund, GPs systematically transfer value from LPs to themselves.

## The Fee Structure Illusion

Co-investments are marketed as zero-fee. This is technically true. No management fee. No carry. But the zero-fee structure obscures the real economics.

When a GP offers co-investment, they are offering LPs a smaller slice of the same deal at the same price. The LP does not get better economics. They get the same economics in smaller size. The GP gets the same economics plus LP capital to deploy without GP commitment.

The zero-fee structure is a marketing device. It creates the impression of LP advantage while actually transferring value. The GP gets more capital to work with. The LP gets a smaller position at unchanged pricing.

## The Due Diligence Gap

Co-investments typically involve abbreviated due diligence. The LP relies on GP due diligence. The LP does not conduct independent analysis. This creates risk.

GP due diligence is optimized for GP decision-making. It focuses on factors relevant to GP investment committee. It does not cover factors relevant to LP portfolio construction. The LP is making an investment decision based on analysis designed for a different decision-maker.

The abbreviated timeline for co-investments further compresses due diligence. LPs often have days to commit capital. This is insufficient to develop independent conviction. The LP is making a material investment decision based on inadequate information.

## The Concentration Risk

Co-investments concentrate portfolio risk. An LP with 100 relationships may receive co-investment opportunities from 20 of those GPs in any given year. The LP cannot allocate to all opportunities. They must select.

Selection among co-investment opportunities creates concentrated positions. The LP ends up with large positions in specific transactions rather than diversified exposure across fund portfolios. The diversification benefit of fund investment is negated.

This concentration risk is rarely analyzed by LPs accepting co-investments. They see the zero-fee structure. They do not see the concentration cost. The net impact on risk-adjusted returns is frequently negative.

## The Recommendation

Co-investments should be evaluated as standalone investments. The question is not whether there is a fee discount. The question is whether the specific transaction offers attractive risk-adjusted returns independent of fee structure.

If the transaction is attractive, the LP should consider whether they can achieve better economics through other structures. Can they invest in the fund at fund-level fees? Can they achieve similar exposure through public markets? Can they access the same opportunity through a different vehicle?

The default should be skepticism. Co-investments are marketed as LP advantage but frequently represent GP advantage. TheLP gets smaller positions at unchanged pricing while the GP gets more capital to deploy. The fee structure is a marketing device, not an LP benefit.
