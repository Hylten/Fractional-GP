Carve-outs are presented as opportunities. A corporate parent sells a division. The division is presumably undervalued within the parent structure. A private equity buyer can unlock value through focus and operational improvement. The narrative is superficially attractive. The track record is not.

## The Selection Problem

Corporate parents sell divisions for reasons. The reasons are not usually that the division is undervalued. The division is usually underperforming relative to parent expectations. Or the division is non-core to strategic priorities. Or the parent needs liquidity.

These reasons correlate with future underperformance. Divisions that underperform within corporate structures tend to continue underperforming. The corporate parent has resources, capital, and management talent that the private equity buyer does not. If the division could not thrive with corporate support, it is unlikely to thrive without it.

The selection bias is severe. The best divisions are not sold. They are retained. They are grown. They are used as platforms for acquisitions. The divisions sold are the ones the corporate parent has decided are not worth keeping. This is adverse selection.

## The Data Speaks

Empirical evidence on carve-out returns is unambiguous. Carve-outs consistently underperform other private equity strategies by 200 to 400 basis points. The underperformance is persistent across vintage years and geographies. It is not a sample size problem. It is a structural problem.

The underperformance has two components. First, acquisition prices for carve-outs are frequently above intrinsic value. Corporate sellers are sophisticated. They hire advisors. They run auctions. The competitive dynamics of the sales process push prices to levels that leave limited upside for buyers.

Second, operational improvement in carve-outs is harder than in other contexts. The divisions being sold often have issues that required corporate parent intervention. Those issues do not disappear in private ownership. They intensify. The private equity owner has fewer resources than the corporate parent to address them.

## The Complexity Trap

Carve-outs are operationally complex. The divested entity typically has shared services agreements with the parent. Transition service agreements are negotiated. IT systems are intertwined. Supply chains are integrated. The complexity creates execution risk and ongoing operational friction.

The transition period is particularly dangerous. The carved-out company must establish capabilities that were previously provided by the parent. This takes time and capital. During the transition, the business is not operating at steady state. Performance typically deteriorates before it improves.

Private equity firms that specialize in carve-outs develop infrastructure to manage this complexity. They have transition teams. They have IT integration capabilities. They have operational experts. But even with specialization, the track record remains underwhelming. The complexity is not manageable at acceptable cost.

## The Pricing Illusion

Carve-out pricing is driven by public market comparables. The seller points to public company multiples. The buyer pays a multiple similar to public comparables. Both parties feel they are doing a rational transaction.

This misses the fundamental point. Public company multiples reflect public company characteristics. Liquidity. Transparency. Governance. Access to capital markets. A private carve-out does not have these characteristics. The multiple should be lower. Usually it is not.

The pricing is further distorted by the auction process. Corporate sellers run competitive processes to maximize price. The competitive dynamic pushes price toward the high end of rational range. Buyers compete away any discount. The winner is usually the buyer who most overestimates the opportunity.

## The Recommendation

Carve-outs should be avoided as a default strategy. The selection bias is adverse. The pricing is distorted. The operational complexity creates execution risk. The track record confirms the structural challenges.

If a carve-out opportunity arises, it should be evaluated with extreme skepticism. The burden of proof is on the buyer to demonstrate why this specific carve-out is different from the population that has consistently underperformed. The default should be pass.

Save capital for platform acquisitions where the ownership structure is cleaner and the improvement potential is clearer. The carve-out narrative is a narrative. The data is not.
