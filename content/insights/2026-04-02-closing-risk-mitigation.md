Deals fail after significant investment in sourcing, evaluation, and negotiation. Buyers who understand closing risks and implement appropriate mitigation achieve higher completion rates. This memo addresses closing risk identification and mitigation.

## The Completion Rate Problem

Mid-market acquisition completion rates range from 50-70% depending on market conditions and buyer sophistication. Failed deals consume resources without producing returns while creating opportunity costs from redirected effort. Improving completion rates directly impacts returns.

Most deal failures occur after significant due diligence investment, meaning the failure cost includes both direct expenses and lost opportunity value. Buyers with high completion rates generate superior returns because they convert sourcing investment into completed acquisitions at higher rates.

## Risk Categories

Closing risks fall into specific categories that require different mitigation approaches.

+ Financing risk: buyer cannot secure required capital. Mitigation involves pre-qualification, financing contingencies, or committed capital.
- Due diligence findings: issues discovered during due diligence that affect transaction willingness. Mitigation involves early issue identification and pricing adjustments.
* Third-party approvals: regulatory, landlord, customer, or other approvals required for closing. Mitigation involves early identification and proactive management.
- Environmental issues: discovered contamination or environmental liability. Mitigation involves early Phase I assessments and appropriate insurance.
- Employee issues: key employee departures or collective bargaining complications. Mitigation involves retention planning and due diligence on labor situation.

Category-specific mitigation approaches reduce completion risk more effectively than generic contingency planning.

## Financing Risk Management

Financing risk is the most common cause of deal failure. Mitigation requires proving financing capability before signing.

+ Get pre-qualified with lenders before signing purchase agreements. Pre-qualification demonstrates capability.
- Structure deals with financing contingencies that protect buyer deposits if financing fails.
* Have multiple financing options available. Relying on single financing sources creates vulnerability.
- Maintain relationships with alternative lenders for backup options.

Pre-qualification is more valuable than price in competitive processes. Sellers prefer buyers with proven financing capability over buyers offering higher prices without financing confirmation.

## Due Diligence Finding Protocols

Due diligence findings that emerge after signing create renegotiation leverage that can derail deals. Protocols for handling findings protect both parties.

+ Establish clear communication protocols for significant findings. Early warning prevents last-minute surprises.
- Create pricing adjustment mechanisms for material findings. Pre-agreed formulas prevent contentious renegotiation.
* Build in appropriate due diligence periods. Compressed timelines increase the risk of findings after closing.

The goal is not to avoid findings but to handle findings in ways that preserve deal economics while maintaining seller relationship.

## Third-Party Approval Management

Third-party approvals create closing dependencies that buyers cannot control. Management requires early identification and proactive process.

+ Map all required third-party approvals early in the process. Missing approvals derail closings.
- Engage third parties early with complete information. Delayed engagement extends timelines.
* Build timeline buffers for approval processes. Regulatory and contractual approvals often take longer than expected.
- Have fallback plans for approvals that cannot be obtained. Alternative structures may preserve deal economics.

Third-party approval risk is often underestimated. Buyers should assume complications and build mitigation into timeline and structure.

## The Contingency Planning Framework

Every significant deal should have contingency plans for identified risks.

+ Financing contingency: defined conditions under which buyer can exit without losing deposit.
- Material adverse change clause: defined conditions that allow buyer to renegotiate or exit.
* Indemnification escrow: holdback structure that protects buyer against unknown liabilities.
- Insurance: specific risk transfer for environmental, title, or other discrete risks.

Contingency planning does not indicate lack of confidence. Rather, it demonstrates professional process management that protects both parties.

## Relationship Preservation

Deal failures damage relationships that have value beyond the specific transaction. Preservation of relationships improves long-term outcomes.

+ Communicate problems early. Surprises destroy trust.
- Propose solutions rather than simply identifying problems. Collaborative problem-solving maintains relationship.
* Accept reasonable compromises. Perfect deals rarely close. Good deals with reasonable terms often create more value than perfect deals that fail.

The same deal that fails due to unreasonable position may succeed with adjustments. Maintaining relationship enables future opportunities.

## Post-Failure Analysis

Failed deals provide learning opportunities that improve future performance. Systematic analysis prevents repeating mistakes.

+ Document failure reason accurately. Self-serving narratives prevent learning.
- Identify process improvements that could prevent similar failures. Not all failures are preventable, but many are.
* Update sourcing criteria if patterns emerge. Certain deal types or situations may have systematically higher failure rates.

The goal is continuous improvement in completion rates through learning from both successes and failures.

## Sammanfattning

Closing risk management directly impacts acquisition returns through completion rate improvement. Financing risk is the most common failure cause and requires pre-qualification and contingency planning. Due diligence findings require communication protocols and pricing adjustment mechanisms. Third-party approvals need early identification and proactive management. Contingency planning protects both parties while demonstrating professional process. Relationship preservation enables future opportunities. Post-failure analysis drives continuous improvement.
