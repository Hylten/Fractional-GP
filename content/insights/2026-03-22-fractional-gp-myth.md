The fractional GP model has been marketed as democratization of private equity access. This framing is marketing, not analysis. The model has structural limitations that produce predictably inferior returns. Understanding these limitations is essential for any LP evaluating fund options.

## The Alignment Problem

Full-skin general partners generate returns because their wealth is at stake. Their incentives are identical toLP incentives. When a GP puts 1% of their net worth into a fund, every decision is colored by that personal exposure. The fraction of a GP's wealth allocated to any single fund is substantially lower than traditional GP commitments.

Consider the math. A GP with 50M in personal wealth committing 1% to a fund produces 500K in skin in the game. That same GP allocating 0.1% across ten different funds produces 50K per fund. The marginal impact of any single fund's performance on the GP's personal wealth is negligible. The behavioral economics shift accordingly.

This is not to suggest fractional GPs act maliciously. They do not. But human decision-making responds to incentive structures. When the downside of a bad decision is absorbed by someone else, the decision-making process changes. Not dramatically. Subtly. And subtlety is the difference between top and bottom quartile performance.

## The Information Asymmetry Gap

Full-time GPs develop proprietary information through immersion. They live in the portfolio. They know the management teams personally. They understand the competitive dynamics of each sector. This information produces edge.

Fractional GPs by definition divide attention. They cannot develop the same depth of knowledge in any single portfolio company. Their information advantage is structurally limited by their model. They see what founders choose to show them. They hear what advisors choose to communicate. They do not develop the independent views that generate true alpha.

The practical implication is that fractional GP portfolios will track indices. They will not beat them. The spread between index performance and active management is entirely attributable to information advantages. Fractional GPs voluntarily surrender that advantage.

## The Fee Layering Effect

Fractional GP models add a fee layer. The platform takes a management fee. The underlying fund takes a management fee. The carry is shared. The cumulative fee drag on net returns is substantially higher than direct fund investment.

This matters because private equity returns have a math problem. The asset class generates returns above public markets by a margin that has been compressi for decades. Fee drag accelerates that compression. Every basis point of unnecessary fee reduces compounding. Fractional GP models add basis points without adding corresponding value.

The math is brutal. A fund that returns 15% gross with 2% fees returns 13% net. Add a fractional GP layer at 1%, and net returns drop to 12%. Over a ten-year holding period, the difference compounds into meaningful wealth destruction for LPs.

## The Selection Bias Trap

Platforms that offer fractional GP access attract a specific type of GP. The best-performing GPs with established track records do not need fractional platforms. They raise traditional funds with traditional fee structures. They have LP relationships that provide access to capital.

The GPs available through fractional platforms are, by selection, those who could not raise capital through traditional channels. They may have insufficient track records. They may have sector expertise that is difficult to validate. They may have personality characteristics that make traditional fundraising challenging.

This is not universally true. Some excellent GPs use fractional platforms for reasons unrelated to fundraising difficulty. But the prior probability that a fractional GP is inferior to a traditional GP is high. And prior probabilities matter in investment decisions.

## The Recommendation

LPs should treat fractional GP platforms as fee drag with negative expected value. The structural disadvantages are not overcome by exceptional individual GPs. The model itself produces expected returns below traditional fund structures.

Save the capital for direct fund allocations or co-investments. Those structures preserve alignment, minimize fee drag, and maintain information advantages. The fractional model serves platform economics, not LP economics.
