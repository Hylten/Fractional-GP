Secondaries are marketed as opportunities to buy private equity at a discount. The discount narrative is compelling but incomplete. Most secondaries are priced at values that do not represent genuine bargains. Understanding the pricing mechanics reveals why most secondaries are still paying full price.

## The Pricing Mechanism

Secondaries are priced as a percentage of net asset value. The NAV is calculated using methodologies that assume continuation of the current trajectory. If a fund is performing at 1.5x multiple, the secondary price will be based on that multiple adjusted for remaining life.

This creates a structural problem. NAV does not mark-to-market. It marks-to-model. The model assumes portfolio companies will continue to generate returns at historical rates. This assumption is frequently wrong. Companies that were growing at 30% may be growing at 5%. The NAV does not reflect the change.

Secondary buyers pay NAV-based prices that embed outdated assumptions. The supposed discount to current market conditions is usually a fiction created by accounting methodologies rather than a genuine alpha opportunity.

## The Selection Problem

Sellers on the secondary market are not random. They sell for reasons. The reasons usually relate to information they have that buyers do not.

Consider what drives secondary sales. LPs sell to rebalance portfolios. This typically happens when a fund is underperforming. Why would an LP hold a winning fund and sell a losing fund? They rebalance because the losing fund represents unknown future risk. The LP has information suggesting the underperformance may continue.

Sellers also include GPs who have received downstream investor pressure to provide liquidity. This typically happens when fund performance is mediocre. Strong performers do not face pressure from downstream investors. They face pressure to deploy more capital.

The net result is that secondary offerings skew toward underperforming funds. The buyer is picking through the remainder of portfolios that original LPs chose to exit. This is not a recipe for outperformance.

## The NAV Lag Problem

Private equity valuations lag public market valuations by twelve to eighteen months. A company purchased at a high multiple may have seen its public market comparable decline substantially before the private equity valuation adjusts.

Secondary buyers acquiring at NAV are buying valuations that may be eighteen months stale. The discount to current market conditions is not 10%. It may be 30% or more. But the discount is hidden in stale NAV rather than visible in the pricing.

This is particularly true in sectors that have experienced compression. Technology multiples have compressed significantly. Many funds still carry technology investments at pre-compression valuations. Secondary buyers paying NAV are not getting a discount. They are buying peak-cycle pricing.

## The J-Curve Amplification

Secondaries are typically purchased in funds with remaining life of three to five years. The J-curve effect is compressed but not eliminated. The buyer pays full price for the vintage year performance without receiving the early-years-of-fund returns that generated that performance.

A fund with a 1.6x multiple has distributed significant returns. A secondary buyer paying 1.4x for that fund is not buying a discount. They are buying the remaining upside potential without the historical upside that produced the current multiple. Their expected return is the fund's remaining upside, not the historical performance.

This math is rarely communicated in secondary marketing materials. The focus is on the 1.4x price versus 1.6x NAV. The focus is not on the expected return from 1.4x to whatever the exit multiple turns out to be.

## The Recommendation

Secondaries can make sense in specific circumstances. When a fund is genuinely mispriced relative to fundamental value. When a forced seller provides liquidity at distressed pricing. When the buyer has superior information about portfolio company trajectories.

The default position should be skepticism. The marketing of secondaries as consistently discounted is designed to move product. The actual discount opportunities are rare, competitive, and usually reserved for large institutional buyers with information advantages.

Save capital for primary fund allocations where the pricing is more transparent and the alignment is clearer. The secondary market is where sellers go to exit. The buyer is usually at an information disadvantage.
