GP commitment is marketed as alignment. The GP invests in their own fund. Their money is at risk. The implication is that this creates identity of interest between GP and LP. The analysis is superficial. The commitment structures do not create the alignment that LPs assume.

## The Commitment Size Problem

GP commitments are typically small relative to fund size. A GP committing 1% to a 500M fund invests 5M. The fund management fee generates 10M annually. The GP earns 10M per year for investing 5M. The annual return on the GP commitment is 200% before any carry.

The GP commitment is not meaningful risk. It is marketing. The GP commitment generates favorable optics without creating genuine alignment. The GP will receive the fee regardless of the commitment outcome.

This creates a perverse incentive. The GP commitment is small enough to be irrelevant to GP decision-making. But it is large enough to create the appearance of alignment. The LP is deceived by the optics while the fundamental misalignment remains.

## The Asymmetric Risk Problem

GP and LP risk exposure is fundamentally asymmetric. LPs risk their entire capital. GPs risk their commitment plus reputation. Reputation risk is theoretically meaningful but practically limited. GPs who underperform do not face career consequences equivalent to LP capital loss.

The asymmetric risk creates decision-making distortions. GPs can take risks that are attractive to them but not attractive to LPs. The GP loses 1% of their net worth if the fund fails. The LP loses 100%. The GP has different risk preferences than the LP.

The carry structure amplifies the asymmetry. GPs receive carry on returns above a hurdle. The carry creates incentive to take risks that increase variance. Higher variance increases the probability of both large wins and large losses. GPs benefit from the wins. LPs bear the full impact of the losses.

## The Commitment Timing Problem

GP commitments are made at fund close. The GP has already received the commitment from LPs. The GP commitment is contingent on LP commitment. There is no scenario where the GP commits and LPs do not.

This creates a false sense of security. The GP commitment appears to be a prerequisite for LP investment. In reality, it is a consequence. The GP commits because they know LPs will commit. The commitment is not a signal of GP conviction. It is a ratification of LP commitment.

The timing also affects the commitment amount. GPs commit amounts that are comfortable given expected fund size. The commitment is calibrated to GP wealth, not to LP interests. The GP does not consider whether their commitment is sufficient to align with LP capital at risk.

## The Waive Problem

Many funds include waiver provisions that allow GPs to reduce or eliminate commitment in certain circumstances. The provisions are designed for legitimate purposes. But they undermine the commitment narrative.

If a GP can waive their commitment under specified conditions, the commitment is not truly at risk. The commitment becomes contingent on favorable conditions. The GP is protected from downside in ways that LPs are not.

The waiver provisions are usually buried in limited partnership agreements. LPs rarely analyze them carefully. The analysis would reveal that GP commitment is more marketing than substance in many cases.

## The Recommendation

GP commitment should not be treated as meaningful alignment. The commitment is usually too small to affect GP decision-making. It is structured to protect GP downside while exposing LP upside. It is timed to follow LP commitment rather than precede it.

LPs should evaluate GPs on the basis of track record, process quality, and incentive structure. The GP commitment is not a meaningful input to the evaluation. It is marketing that should be discounted.

The focus should be on GP behavior. Do they make decisions that are consistent with long-term LP interest? Do they avoid risks that transfer value from LPs to GPs? Do they communicate transparently about portfolio company performance? These behavioral factors predict future alignment more reliably than commitment size.
