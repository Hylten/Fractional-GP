## The Mid-Market’s Structural Problem: Broker Auctions Distort Value

Everyone knows it.  
No one admits it.  
Pricing spirals upward.  
Discipline spirals downward.  

The mid-market—defined loosely as companies generating $3 million to $50 million in EBITDA—has become dependent on broker-led processes. These processes were designed for *throughput*, not discernment. When a broker assembles 150 to 300 NDA signatories, distributes the CIM, and kicks off a competitive sprint to LOI, it creates artificial scarcity and price tension.

This model rewards every stakeholder *except* the investor:

• Brokers win through velocity and fees.  
• Sellers win through inflated valuations.  
• Advisors win through expanded scopes.  
• Investors absorb the mispricing.

Traditional auctions do the one thing a disciplined buyer should avoid: they systematically devalue prudence. A rational buyer who insists on a DSCR above 2.0x, clean working capital cycles, and confirmable customer concentration dynamics is simply outbid by someone with a more aggressive capital stack. That someone is often not the best manager, nor the best steward of the business; they are simply the most optimistic underwriter.

Optimism prices poorly. Prudence prices well.

In this environment, underwriting teams lose their defensive compass. Instead of asking, “Is this asset worth owning for ten years?” they are conditioned to ask, “What will win the auction?” This behavioral shift is the quiet decay at the core of modern private equity.

## Why Auctions Fail: A Clinical Diagnosis

Let’s articulate the pathology clearly:

1. **Information Asymmetry Favors Speed over Understanding**  
   In a crowded auction, diligence is constrained. Winning bidders often *hope* for post-LOI confirmatory clarification. Hope is not underwriting.

2. **Competitive Tension Inflates Valuations Beyond Intrinsic Value**  
   Even disciplined investors find themselves pushing leverage, trimming capex budgets, or inflating forward EBITDA to remain competitive. This is not value creation; it is value hallucination.

3. **Time Pressure Destroys Analytical Depth**  
   True underwriting requires patient pattern recognition. Rushed underwriting creates blind spots, which eventually become impairments.

4. **Misaligned Incentives Produce Systematic Overpricing**  
   Brokers want the highest price. Investors want the right price. These objectives are mutually exclusive.

5. **The Process Rewards Generalists, Not Stewards**  
   Auctions disproportionately reward firms that can simply afford to swing hardest. Stewardship—the Christian principle of caring responsibly for what one is entrusted with—is sidelined.

Good companies are consumed.  
Good judgment is compromised.  
Good capital is misallocated.  
Good stewardship is ignored.  

The system is not broken; it is misaligned. Misalignment is worse because it masquerades as efficiency.
