Generalist deal sourcing attempts to cover all sectors with all strategies. The approach maximizes optionality but minimizes effectiveness. Sector-specific origination creates advantages that generalists cannot replicate regardless of resources.

## The Specialization Premium

Sectors have unique dynamics, trigger events, and advisor networks. The firm that understands sector-specific patterns sees opportunities that generalists miss.

* Healthcare services deal flow is driven by regulatory change. New compliance requirements trigger owner assessments of scale-or-exit decisions. The timing window is narrow. Relationships with industry consultants and compliance attorneys provide access.

* Industrial distribution deal flow is driven by succession planning. Family-owned businesses face demographic reality. The founding generation retires. Children are uninterested. Advisors who serve this community hold the relationships.

* Technology-enabled services deal flow is driven by founder dynamics. Burnout. Market consolidation. Plateau decisions. These trigger events create opportunities at specific moments.

The specialization premium comes from understanding these sector-specific dynamics deeply enough to predict when opportunities emerge and react faster than competitors.

## The Network Concentration

Sector focus concentrates advisor networks. The firm that focuses on healthcare sees the same advisors repeatedly. The firm that spans sectors meets different advisors for each deal.

* Concentrated networks build deeper relationships.
* Repeated interactions build trust.
* Trust generates referrals.
* Referrals generate proprietary flow.

The generalist network is broad but shallow. The specialist network is narrow but deep. The depth creates advantages that breadth cannot match.

## The Signal Processing Advantage

Sector specialization improves signal processing. The same signal means different things in different sectors.

> A business listed for sale might indicate distress in one sector and strategic repositioning in another. The interpretation requires sector expertise.

> An owner approaching retirement might signal succession planning in family businesses and exit timing in founder-led ventures. The response requires sector understanding.

> A regulatory change might create opportunity in regulated industries and threat in others. The implication requires sector context.

The specialized firm processes signals with sector context. The generalist processes signals without context. The contextual processing produces better decisions.

## The Credibility Multiplier

Sector focus builds credibility. The buyer who demonstrates deep sector expertise gains trust more easily than the generalist buyer.

* Sector-specific track record proves capability.
* Sector-specific vocabulary demonstrates knowledge.
* Sector-specific references provide social proof.
* Sector-specific relationships open doors.

The credibility multiplier makes deals easier to close. The owner trusts the specialist more than the generalist. The trust accelerates negotiation and improves terms.

## The Resource Allocation Decision

Resource allocation determines whether a firm can achieve sector specialization. The allocation decision has trade-offs.

+ Specialized teams have deeper expertise but narrower opportunity sets.
+ Generalist teams have broader coverage but shallower expertise.
+ The optimal balance depends on market conditions and firm capabilities.

Most firms claim specialization but execute generalist strategies. The gap between claim and execution creates opportunity for firms that actually specialize.

The decision is strategic, not tactical. Choose the sector. Build the expertise. Develop the network. Capture the advantage.
