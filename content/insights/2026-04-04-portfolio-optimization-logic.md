Acquisition programs should be managed as portfolios rather than individual transactions. Buyers who understand portfolio dynamics optimize returns across their entire acquisition program. This memo addresses portfolio optimization logic.

## The Portfolio Perspective

Individual deal analysis focuses on transaction-level returns. Portfolio analysis focuses on program-level returns and risk. The perspectives produce different insights and different decisions.

A portfolio perspective recognizes that some deals will exceed expectations while others will underperform. The distribution of outcomes matters more than any single outcome. Portfolio optimization seeks to maximize expected returns given the distribution of potential outcomes.

The portfolio perspective also recognizes synergies across deals that individual analysis misses. Cross-selling opportunities, operational leverage, and market positioning create value that accrues to the portfolio rather than individual companies.

## Return Distribution Dynamics

Acquisition returns follow power law distributions. A small number of deals generate most of the returns. A larger number of deals generate moderate returns. Some deals lose money.

The return distribution implies that portfolio construction matters more than individual deal selection. Maximizing the probability of including high-return deals matters more than minimizing the probability of losing money on any single deal.

The practical implication: spread effort across multiple deal opportunities rather than concentrating on single deals. The portfolio benefits from optionality.

## Sector Allocation Decisions

Portfolio sector allocation affects both returns and risk.

+ Concentrated sector portfolios: deeper expertise, operational synergies, market timing advantages. Higher risk of sector-specific shocks.
- Diversified sector portfolios: reduced idiosyncratic risk, broader opportunity access, less operational leverage. Lower returns from any single sector.

The optimal allocation depends on market conditions, buyer expertise, and risk tolerance. Most acquirers should maintain moderate concentration (2-3 sectors) rather than extreme concentration or diversification.

## Size Segment Allocation

Different size segments have different return and risk characteristics.

+ Lower-middle-market (5-15M EBITDA): higher operational involvement, more value creation opportunity, less competition.
- Upper-middle-market (15-50M EBITDA): lower operational involvement, institutional competitive dynamics, more capital requirements.

Portfolio allocation across size segments affects returns and operational demands. Buyers should consider their operational capacity when allocating across segments.

## Staging and Timing

Portfolio construction involves staging decisions about timing and concentration over time.

+ Concentrated deployment: deploying capital quickly into multiple deals. Higher short-term risk, captures market opportunity.
- Staged deployment: spacing acquisitions over time. Lower short-term risk, potentially misses market opportunity.

Market conditions affect optimal staging. Strong markets with abundant opportunities favor concentrated deployment. Uncertain markets favor staged deployment.

## Return Correlation

Portfolio returns depend on correlation across deals.

+ Correlated deals: deals that rise and fall together. Similar sectors, similar geographic regions, similar business models. Higher portfolio volatility.
- Uncorrelated deals: deals with independent performance dynamics. Different sectors, different regions, different business models. Lower portfolio volatility.

Correlation management enables risk-adjusted return optimization. Low correlation across deals reduces portfolio volatility without necessarily reducing expected returns.

## Scaling Considerations

Portfolio optimization changes as acquisition programs scale.

+ Early stage: concentrate on building track record and expertise. Fewer, larger bets may be appropriate.
- Mid stage: diversify to reduce risk and access more opportunities. More, smaller positions.
- Mature stage: optimize return distribution. Consider co-investments and syndication.

The appropriate portfolio strategy evolves as programs mature. Early-stage programs should accept higher concentration risk to build expertise. Mature programs can optimize diversification.

## Portfolio Rebalancing

Active portfolio management involves rebalancing as conditions change.

+ Add to winners: companies exceeding expectations may warrant additional investment.
- Prune losers: companies underperforming expectations may warrant exit or restructuring.
- Sector shifts: market conditions may warrant changing sector allocation.
- Size shifts: capital availability may warrant changing size segment allocation.

Rebalancing should be systematic but not reactive to short-term fluctuations. Time horizon matters for rebalancing decisions.

## Sammanfattning

Portfolio perspective provides insights that individual deal analysis misses. Return distributions follow power laws, making portfolio construction more important than individual deal selection. Sector allocation and size segment allocation affect returns and risk. Staging and timing decisions depend on market conditions. Return correlation across deals determines portfolio volatility. Portfolio optimization changes as programs scale. Rebalancing should be systematic rather than reactive.
