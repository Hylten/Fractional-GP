Due diligence in mid-market acquisitions often becomes a sinkhole of time and money that produces insufficient return on investment. Buyers who architect systematic due diligence processes evaluate opportunities efficiently while avoiding analysis paralysis. This memo outlines the due diligence architecture.

## The Due Diligence Cost Structure

Due diligence consumes resources that directly reduce acquisition returns. Legal fees, accounting fees, and opportunity costs of management time spent on evaluation rather than operations accumulate rapidly. The average mid-market due diligence process costs 1-3% of transaction value in direct fees plus significant indirect costs.

Traditional due diligence approaches assume that comprehensive analysis produces better outcomes. The assumption is frequently false. Most due diligence findings either confirm known issues or identify problems that would not change the investment decision. The incremental information from extensive due diligence rarely justifies the incremental cost.

The solution is not less due diligence but better due diligence. Systematic architecture focuses resources on high-signal areas while accepting appropriate uncertainty in lower-risk dimensions. The architecture approach produces superior risk-adjusted returns by optimizing the information-cost tradeoff.

## The Tiered Evaluation Framework

Due diligence should operate in tiers that allocate resources based on decision impact.

+ Tier 1: Issues that would prevent closing or materially change terms. Key man dependence, environmental liability, pending litigation, regulatory compliance gaps. Focus intensive resources here.
- Tier 2: Issues that affect valuation or post-closing planning. Working capital adjustments, customer concentration, supplier relationships, operational gaps. Allocate moderate resources.
* Tier 3: Issues that affect integration planning but not valuation. Cultural alignment, system compatibility, redundant functions. Light touch assessment.

The tiered approach concentrates expensive due diligence on genuinely decision-critical areas. Tier 1 findings can kill deals or renegotiate terms. Tier 2 findings affect price negotiation. Tier 3 findings inform integration but rarely affect the fundamental decision to acquire.

## Financial Due Diligence Priorities

Financial due diligence typically receives disproportionate attention relative to its decision value. The systematic approach identifies the high-signal financial items and deprioritizes standard comprehensive analysis.

+ Cash flow verification: confirm that EBITDA calculations reflect actual cash generation. Adjust for non-recurring items, owner expenses, and aggressive accounting policies.
- Working capital analysis: understand the true working capital requirement and normalize for owner manipulation. Look for revenue recognition timing, inventory management, and payables optimization.
* Revenue quality assessment: evaluate revenue sustainability through customer retention analysis, pricing trends, and competitive positioning. Recurring revenue businesses warrant different treatment than project-based businesses.
- Margin analysis: decompose margins by product line, customer segment, and channel. Identify improvement opportunities and sustainability risks.

The financial due diligence priority is understanding what cash the business will generate, not confirming every line item matches the seller's representations.

## Legal Due Diligence Scope

Legal due diligence in mid-market acquisitions should focus on liability identification rather than comprehensive review.

+ Litigation assessment: identify actual and potential litigation exposure. Prioritize pending matters and recent settlements.
- Contract review: focus on material contracts with customers, suppliers, and key employees. Standard contracts receive minimal attention.
* Regulatory compliance: assess regulatory exposure in heavily regulated sectors. Environmental, healthcare, and financial services require intensive focus.
- Entity and ownership: verify ownership structure, entity maintenance, and historical compliance.

The legal due diligence scope should match risk profile. Regulated businesses require extensive regulatory due diligence. Simple service businesses with owner management require lighter touch.

## Operational Due Diligence Value

Operational due diligence often produces the highest value creation insights in mid-market acquisitions. The focus should be on improvement opportunity identification rather than verification of seller representations.

+ Key personnel assessment: identify critical employees, retention risk, and replacement costs. Owner-dependent businesses carry specific risk that requires assessment.
- Systems evaluation: understand technology infrastructure, integration requirements, and upgrade investment needs. Legacy systems create integration friction.
* Process efficiency: assess operational processes against best practice. Identify improvement opportunities that the buyer can execute post-acquisition.
- Customer concentration: understand revenue concentration and relationship depth. High customer concentration requires specific risk assessment and mitigation planning.

Operational due diligence produces the insights that drive value creation post-acquisition. The focus should be forward-looking improvement potential rather than backward-looking verification.

## The Red Flag Framework

Due diligence should systematically evaluate common value destruction patterns.

+ Customer concentration above 30%: single customer dependence creates material risk.
- Revenue decline trajectory: negative trends require understanding cause and reversibility.
* Key person dependence: owner knowledge concentrated in single individual creates transition risk.
- Regulatory or legal exposure: pending matters or compliance gaps create liability risk.
- Working capital inflation: owner-optimized working capital creates post-closing cash flow issues.

Red flag identification triggers deeper analysis in specific areas rather than comprehensive review. The framework enables efficient allocation of due diligence resources to highest-risk areas.

## Process Architecture

Due diligence process architecture enables efficient execution.

+ Stage-gated process: complete initial due diligence before committing to comprehensive analysis. Gate each stage with decision criteria.
- Data room structure: organize information request systematically to enable efficient review. Avoid fishing expeditions.
* Specialist engagement: engage specialists for high-complexity areas. Generalists cannot provide expert-level assessment.
- Timeline management: set aggressive but realistic timelines. Extended due diligence increases cost without proportional information gain.

The due diligence architecture should produce a decision within 4-8 weeks for typical mid-market transactions. Extended processes signal either seller reluctance or buyer inefficiency.

## Sammanfattning

Due diligence architecture enables efficient evaluation that focuses resources on decision-critical areas. The tiered framework allocates intensive analysis to high-impact items while accepting appropriate uncertainty in lower-risk dimensions. Financial, legal, and operational due diligence each have specific priorities that differ from traditional comprehensive approaches. The red flag framework identifies high-risk patterns that trigger deeper analysis. Systematic due diligence produces superior risk-adjusted returns by optimizing the information-cost tradeoff.
