Capital Structure Optimization in Acquisition Financing

April 1, 2026 • Jonas Hyltén

Capital structure decisions in acquisitions significantly impact risk-adjusted returns. Buyers who optimize capital structure achieve superior results compared to those who default to conventional financing approaches. This memo analyzes capital structure optimization strategies.

The Leverage Multiplier

Debt leverage amplifies returns in both directions. Moderate leverage enhances equity returns without excessive risk. High leverage creates return volatility that destroys value during downturns. The optimal capital structure balances return enhancement against risk of distress.

The mathematics of leverage are straightforward. With 50% debt and 50% equity, a business generating 10% pre-interest returns produces 15% equity returns after interest expense. With 70% debt and 30% equity, the same 10% returns produce 23% equity returns. The leverage multiplier works in both directions. Returns decline with leverage during downturns.

Mid-market acquisitions typically support 3-4x EBITDA debt loads without excessive distress risk. Higher leverage requires specific value creation plans and margin for error.

Debt Structure Options

Multiple debt structure options affect risk and return differently.

+ Senior bank debt: lowest cost, most restrictive covenants, first lien position. Appropriate for stable cash flow businesses.

- Subordinated debt: higher cost, more flexible terms, second lien position. Appropriate when senior capacity is insufficient.

* Seller notes: often the most flexible financing, subordinate to senior debt. Creates seller alignment and reduces total financing requirements.

- Mezzanine debt: hybrid debt-equity instruments with warrants or conversion features. Higher cost but flexible structure.

Optimal debt structure often involves multiple layers that collectively provide required capital at appropriate cost and risk levels.

Equity Contribution Decisions

Equity contribution determines leverage ratio and risk exposure. The decision involves tradeoff between return enhancement and distress risk.

+ Minimum equity: SBA and conventional lenders often require 20-30% equity. This minimum establishes baseline leverage.

- Additional equity: buyers can contribute additional equity beyond minimum requirements. Additional equity reduces risk but also reduces leverage returns.

* Seller equity: rollover equity from sellers creates alignment and reduces buyer equity requirements. Seller equity also provides upside participation.

The equity decision should reflect confidence in cash flow stability and risk tolerance. Conservative buyers contribute more equity. Aggressive buyers maximize leverage.

Cash Flow Coverage Analysis

Debt service coverage determines sustainable leverage levels. The analysis should stress test against downside scenarios.

+ Debt service coverage ratio: EBITDA divided by annual debt service. Conservative minimum is 1.25x.

- Interest coverage: EBITDA divided by annual interest expense. Minimum 2x provides operating flexibility.

* Fixed charge coverage: EBITDA minus capex divided by debt service plus fixed costs. Accounts for ongoing investment needs.

The coverage analysis should incorporate realistic downside scenarios. Optimistic projections do not support leverage decisions. Conservative scenarios establish appropriate debt levels.

Covenant Structure Impact

Debt covenants restrict operational flexibility but provide lenders comfort that enables financing. Covenant structure significantly affects post-acquisition operations.

+ Maintenance covenants: tested periodically based on financial performance. EBITDA, revenue, or liquidity thresholds.

- Incurrence covenants: tested only when taking specific actions like additional borrowing or distributions.

* Financial reporting: regular reporting requirements create discipline but also administrative burden.

Covenant negotiation should balance lender requirements against operational flexibility needs. Aggressive covenant packages create ongoing risk. Conservative covenants provide operating room.

Refinancing Risk

Acquisition debt often carries refinancing risk that affects return calculations. Bridge financing, variable rate debt, or short-term facilities require eventual refinancing or repayment.

+ Refinancing risk increases when markets are volatile or credit conditions tighten.

- Term loan A structures amortize over 5-7 years, reducing refinancing risk.

* Extension options provide flexibility when refinancing markets are unfavorable.

The refinancing risk analysis should incorporate market conditions at acquisition and potential future scenarios. Refinancing risk can destroy returns if not properly managed.

Return Optimization Framework

Capital structure decisions should optimize risk-adjusted returns rather than minimize cost or maximize leverage.

+ Model returns across leverage scenarios. Calculate equity returns under base, upside, and downside cases.

- Evaluate flexibility implications. Highly levered structures restrict operational flexibility.

* Consider strategic optionality. Less leverage preserves acquisition capacity for future deals.

The optimization process should produce capital structure that maximizes expected risk-adjusted returns while preserving flexibility for operational challenges and future opportunities.
