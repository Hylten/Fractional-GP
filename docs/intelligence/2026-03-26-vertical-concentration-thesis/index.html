Vertical Concentration Thesis: Why Sector Focus Beats Diversification

March 26, 2026 • Jonas Hyltén

The diversification argument in acquisition strategy sounds sophisticated but fails under scrutiny. Buyers who spread capital across sectors accumulate diluted returns. Vertical concentration generates compounding advantages that diversify portfolios cannot replicate. This memo outlines the thesis for sector-focused acquisition programs.

The Diversification Delusion

Portfolio theory from capital markets does not translate directly to private company acquisitions. Public market diversification reduces idiosyncratic risk through statistical independence of securities. Private company acquisitions do not exhibit the same independence properties. Economic cycles, sector-specific dynamics, and supply chain interdependencies create correlation structures that undermine diversification benefits.

A buyer acquiring businesses across three sectors faces three distinct sets of competitive dynamics, regulatory environments, and operational improvement opportunities. The cognitive load of maintaining expertise across sectors dilutes the deep knowledge that generates superior returns. The theoretical risk reduction from diversification comes at the cost of return reduction from diluted expertise.

The empirical evidence is unambiguous. Sector-focused acquirers outperform diversified acquirers by 300-500 basisives annually in the mid-market. The spread originates from better deal selection, operational improvement capability, and exit timing. All three factors depend on deep sector knowledge that diversification precludes.

Knowledge Compounding

Deep sector focus creates knowledge that compounds across deals. A buyer who acquires five businesses in HVAC services develops operational expertise that applies to each subsequent acquisition. They understand the technician labor market, the equipment supply chain, the maintenance revenue characteristics, and the customer acquisition dynamics specific to that sector. This accumulated knowledge reduces execution risk and improves integration outcomes.

Each subsequent acquisition in the same sector benefits from prior experience. The learning curve flattens, due diligence becomes more efficient, and operational improvement opportunities become more apparent. The compounding effect produces returns that accelerate over time rather than mean-reverting.

Diversified acquirers restart the learning curve with each new sector entry. They pay the full cost of developing expertise without the benefit of accumulated knowledge. The efficiency penalty compounds across the portfolio, creating persistent underperformance relative to focused competitors.

Deal Flow Advantages

Sector-focused acquirers generate superior deal flow through credibility and network effects. Owners in a specific sector talk to each other. When a buyer completes a successful acquisition in the sector, word spreads through industry networks. Subsequent sellers view the buyer as a credible, serious acquirer rather than a speculative financial buyer.

The credibility advantage manifests in multiple ways. Sellers in the sector are more likely to take meetings with a known quantity. Advisors in the sector recommend the buyer to clients. Bankers structure financing around the buyer's known parameters. The entire transaction ecosystem becomes more efficient for a focused buyer.

Brokers prioritize focused buyers for their off-market deals. A broker with a motivated seller in HVAC services will call the HVAC-focused buyer before calling a diversified buyer with broader but shallower sector relationships. The broker knows the focused buyer can evaluate quickly, close cleanly, and complete integrations that create successful track records, which generates future referrals.

Operational Leverage

Operating improvements in private company acquisitions depend on transferring best practices across portfolio companies. A diversified acquirer cannot transfer operational practices because the practices do not apply across sectors. A sector-focused acquirer can implement proven operational frameworks from one acquisition to the next.

Consider the value creation opportunity in standardization. A focused acquirer in healthcare services can implement standardized billing systems, compliance frameworks, and patient acquisition processes across all portfolio companies. The standardization investment benefits from full portfolio application rather than partial application to a single company.

The talent market advantages similarly compound. A sector-focused acquirer becomes a known employer in the sector's labor market, enabling better recruiting for portfolio companies. The acquirer understands sector-specific compensation structures, career pathways, and talent availability in ways that diversified acquirers cannot replicate.

Competitive Positioning

Focused acquirers can establish dominant positions within their sectors. The combination of deep expertise, proven credibility, and operational leverage creates competitive moats that diversified competitors cannot cross. A sector-focused buyer can become the natural acquirer of choice for any seller in that sector.

The competitive advantage extends to capital markets. Investors recognize sector-focused strategies and assign premium valuations to focused acquirers. The track record of operational improvements within a known sector reduces perceived risk, enabling better financing terms and higher leverage.

Strategic buyers in the sector view focused financial acquirers as potential exit destinations rather than competitors. This dynamic creates optionality for portfolio companies, whether through eventual strategic sale or continued financial ownership with operational improvement.

Implementation Framework

Building a focused acquisition program requires specific structural choices.

+ Select 1-2 sectors based on personal expertise, existing network, and market opportunity.

* Build sector-specific advisor networks: attorneys, accountants, bankers who serve the sector.

+ Develop sector-specific sourcing engines that identify targets based on sector dynamics.

- Create operational improvement frameworks specific to the sector's value drivers.

* Hire sector expertise into the acquisition team rather than expecting generic operators to develop sector knowledge.

The focus discipline requires resisting attractive opportunities outside the chosen sectors. The opportunity cost of passing on out-of-sector deals is real but manageable. The returns from focus exceed the option value of diversification.

Risk Considerations

Sector concentration introduces concentration risk. Economic downturns affecting the sector will impact the entire portfolio. This risk is real but manageable through careful attention to sector cyclicality and portfolio construction within the sector.

The appropriate mitigation is sector selection rather than diversification. Choosing sectors with defensive characteristics (recession resilience, recurring revenue, essential services) reduces concentration risk without sacrificing focus benefits. A buyer focused on healthcare services or essential infrastructure faces lower cyclical risk than a buyer focused on discretionary consumer services.

Acquiring through sector cycles also provides risk-adjusted returns. The best sector-focused acquirers accelerate acquisition activity during sector downturns when valuations decline and motivated sellers increase. The cyclical timing skill requires deep sector knowledge that focused acquirers develop.

Sammanfattning

Vertical concentration produces superior returns through knowledge compounding, deal flow advantages, operational leverage, and competitive positioning. The diversification argument fails because private company acquisitions do not exhibit the statistical independence that makes diversification valuable in public markets. Implementation requires sector selection based on expertise and opportunity, followed by disciplined focus that resists diversification pressure. The returns from focus consistently exceed the returns from diversification in mid-market acquisitions.
