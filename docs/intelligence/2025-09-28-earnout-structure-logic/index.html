Earnout Structure Logic: Aligning Incentives Post-Acquisition

April 11, 2026 • Jonas Hyltén

Earnouts align seller and buyer incentives post-acquisition. Properly structured earnouts create value; poorly structured earnouts create disputes. This memo analyzes earnout structure logic.

The Earnout Rationale

Earnouts transfer portion of purchase price contingent on future performance.

+ Bridge valuation gaps between buyer and seller expectations.

+ Align seller incentives for post-acquisition performance.

+ Reduce buyer risk through contingent payments.

+ Create seller retention through performance incentives.

Earnouts solve problems that pure cash transactions cannot solve. They enable deals that would otherwise fail.

Earnout Structure Components

Earnout structures have specific components that require design.

+ Performance metric: EBITDA, revenue, gross profit, or other measurable metric.

+ Measurement period: typically 1-3 years post-closing.

+ Payment structure: annual payments, lump sum at end, or tiered structure.

+ Maximum and floor: cap and floor on earnout amounts.

+ Governance: who measures performance, how disputes resolved.

Each component affects alignment and risk allocation.

Metric Selection

Earnout metric selection should align with value creation drivers.

+ EBITDA: appropriate when buyer can influence profitability directly.

+ Revenue: appropriate when top-line is primary value driver.

+ Gross profit: appropriate when margin is primary concern.

+ Custom metrics: appropriate for specific value creation opportunities.

Metric selection should match business economics and buyer operational control.

Measurement Challenges

Earnout measurement creates disputes when not carefully structured.

+ Accounting policy differences: buyer and seller accounting may differ.

+ One-time events: unusual items distort measurement.

+ Acquisition effects: buyer acquisitions distort comparison.

+ External factors: market conditions affect performance.

Structure should minimize measurement dispute potential.

Common Dispute Patterns

Earnout disputes commonly arise from specific situations.

+ Aggressive accounting: buyer applies aggressive accounting to minimize earnout.

+ Under-investment: buyer under-invests in earnout business to minimize payments.

+ Key person departure: seller leaves, reducing business performance.

+ Market conditions: external factors beyond either party affect performance.

Anticipating disputes enables preventive structure.

Dispute Prevention

Earnout structure should prevent disputes through clear terms.

+ Define accounting policies explicitly in purchase agreement.

+ Create adjustment mechanisms for acquisition effects and one-time items.

+ Include key employee retention requirements.

+ Establish independent accounting for disputes.

Clear terms prevent disputes; vague terms create litigation.

The Retention Angle

Earnouts often serve retention purposes beyond value transfer.

+ Key employee earnouts retain critical personnel.

+ Seller employment agreements tie earnout to continued employment.

+ Non-compete provisions align post-closing behavior.

Retention considerations should inform earnout structure.
